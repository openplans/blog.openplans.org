Knight News Challenge: staff picks

Posted on March 18, 2013 by Frank Hebbert

Like many of our peers, we’ve been spending the last few days reading and commenting on entries to the Knight News Challenge, an amazing selection of civic technology projects. Here are a few of our “staff picks”. Enjoy.

(Obviously, modestly prevents us from picking our own submissions – but they are both worth checking out, and will be even better with your comments and criticism:

  • Plan In A Box: Engaging in neighborhood change is empowering and creates better places to live and work. Plan In A Box provides a one-click toolbox for city staff to promote projects and gather input with effective websites. More.
  • Making Meetings Matter: Public meetings are important, but our tools to promote them are stuck in a previous age. By changing how people discover meetings through new web tools and data, we’ll empower communities to be more involved in local decision-making. More.

And without further ado, our staff picks…)

Picking Celebrating Small Acts of Great Public Service Can Change Everything, Aaron says –

Having worked with directly with civil servants for many years, particularly through Code for America, I know how many thoughtful, compassionate people are working hard to make out governments work effectively. Plauditor proposes to give citizens a way to sharing “wow stories” of great interactions with these amazing public workers. There are many challenges to overcome for this to be effective, but I’m a hopeless optimist and love to someone is challenging the current negative stereotypes.

 

Picking Why Don’t We Own This? Detroit, Jeff says –

I’m excited to see this project succeed because it’s a materialist view of the people and structures that are currently changing Detroit. The project acknowledges the performativity of land-use and ownership; empty lots, for instance, are simultaneously part of the problem and (a potential) solution. But who is responsible for the current state of these lots, and what other connections or motives might be circumscribed using this ownership information? I’m worried there isn’t enough awareness of the players (especially emerging ones) in Detroit’s real-estate market, and to the extent that this awareness fosters a conversation (ahead of big changes) around who the true power brokers are (and will be) in Detroit, this project can be a very important part of ensuring Detroiters are part of the changes currently happening in their community.

 

Picking State Financial Data at Your Fingertips, Anthony says –

State Data Lab has the potential to be a really great app. It gives people a quick, centralized way to crunch and compare financial, demographic and statistical data on individual states and the United States as a whole. It’s going to really buzz years and decades in the future when today’s open data initiatives produce a trove of information to look back upon. Having a platform like this in place and populated with yesterday’s historical data will be just plain cool.

 

Mjumbe picked Planning meets Popular Mechanics, The Community Planner: a hands-on journal for citizens & planners (also a fave of mine). He comments –

The most difficult step in having an impact on a community is often knowing what to do and how to do it. The Community Planner is an online journal for sharing planning tools and techniques, empowering citizens and community groups to learn about the nuts and bolts of planning and the planning process. The articles are designed to be hands-on and to provide citizens, staff, and elected officials with the tools and techniques they need to be successful at developing open and community driven approaches to planning and government.

 

Jordan writes about his pick, Closed Loop: A cutting-edge transparency tool for investigative journalists -

I think this is a great tool that can unlock the investigative promise of open data sets. It’s being proposed by a team in Chicago that’s got experience working on these sorts of problems, and their approach and software can be implemented in cities and states across the nation. In an economic environment that’s hostile to hard-hitting long form stories, this is a vital tool for empowering young journalists.

 

Picking Procure.io, Ellen says –

I’ve worked for small business government contractors for nearly a decade, and one thing that’s always frustrating is the opacity of the bid, review, and award process. Businesses should spend their time perfecting technology and products– not becoming experts in procurement systems that differ between levels of government, and frequently within them as well. Simpler and more transparent procurement will make it possible for more businesses to compete for work and easier for agencies to make good decisions, and help to ensure that taxpayers are getting the best value for their money.

 

And finally, a great project from Phil Ashlock, an alum of OpenPlans — “DemocracyMap is a simple website that allows people to find and contact all the governing bodies and elected officials that represent them from the most local office to the national level”. Read more about it.
